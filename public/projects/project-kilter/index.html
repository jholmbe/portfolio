project: kilter

minimalist spreadsheet application for fitness
Check out the REPOSITORY
project: kilter is a design project for UMD's INST451 class. The professor requested the class to design for a personal passion; my focus was weightlifting. Also, teaching staff provided mentorship during our 5 design sprints in the semester (~12 weeks). The objective of the class was UI/UX and not the programming of the interfaces.

Weightlifters with 2+ years experience lack tools truly optimized for their workflow. Spreadsheets and plain-text methods are functional and customizable, but weren't designed with weightlifters in mind. project: kilter seeks to optimize current methods of tracking personal fitness by offering a user-friendly interface, modular features, and leveraging the community through the plugins system.

FINAL UI

This was an independent project. I developed a lightweight spreadsheet application with the guidance of real users. The product niche is the modularity of the plugins system, enabling lifters to choose their workflow. In combination, there is cross-platform compatibility (desktop & mobile), 3 included togglable plugins, and developer support. The minimalist design is inspired by my desire to declutter modern fitness interfaces, and I like the color green.

USERS, TRENDS, AND EXPLORATION

The first two sprints were exploratory. In Sprint 1, I focused on defining the scope: identifying a real problem, speaking directly with potential users, and conducting market research to understand both user pain points and competitor trends. I synthesized this input into clear project goals and design principles. Sprint 2 was about translating these insights into structure. I sketched early ideas, mapped out the user journey, and built the information architecture. This gave me a foundation to test assumptions early and establish a strong direction going into later iterations.

ITERATIVE PRACTICE

With a clear direction, Sprints 3-5 were dedicated to testing, refining, and improving. Biweekly, I gathered feedback from users and mentors on usability and UI details. Every design decision was based on values from the users, such as simplicity, efficiency, and usability. I stuck to a defined feature list to stay user-centric. Throughout the process, I held my work to a high personal standard, continually asking: Does this design solve the problem in the most intuitive way possible?

REFLECTION

The project is public so feel free to take a look at my work. This was my first Figma project so I was learning the software itself alongside design practices. I'm incredibly happy with this project however if I had to do it again, I'd define a smaller scope instead of re-invent the wheel. This sort of turned out as a productivity tool and might be hard to implement with my amatuer programming skills.
